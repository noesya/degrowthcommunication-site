Degrowth policies cover many aspects of society and our economy. There are hundreds of proposed policies, that could all work together to bring about a degrowth transition. To navigate this diversity, we're following the excellent example set out by a paper that mapped 530 policies and structured our content around 13 thematic areas, each reflecting a critical aspect of degrowth. 

Further, to provide clarity and direction, we've categorized policies into two key dimensions:

Policy Ends (Goals, Objectives, Targets): This dimension focuses on the 'what'—the outcomes we aim to achieve through degrowth. These goals guide our vision and outline the desired end states that reflect our values and priorities in the degrowth movement.

Policy Means (Methods, Instruments, Calibration): Here, we address the 'how'—the tools, methods, and processes we employ to achieve our degrowth objectives. This includes a range of policy instruments and interventions designed to address specific challenges and opportunities within each thematic area.

This will allow for a more meaningful understanding of both the objectives of degrowth and the practical steps required to realize them.

Increasing Well-being Without Economic Growth

  • Universal Basic Services: Guarantee public access to essential services, including education, healthcare, public transport, housing, food, and essential utilities to ensure a high quality of life independent of economic growth.
  • Reduced Workweek: Shorten the workweek to 3-4 days to distribute employment more equitably, aiming to decrease unemployment and give people more free time.
  • Job Guarantee: Create a federally funded but locally administered employment program offering jobs to all, focusing on projects that yield social and environmental benefits, ensuring meaningful employment opportunities.
  • Basic Income: Establish a guaranteed minimum income for individuals unable to work or unemployed (looking at you, AI), securing a dignified standard of living for everyone.
  • Debt Cancellation: Push for the cancellation of debts, particularly for countries in the Global South, to relieve economic constraints and allow for much-needed investment in public services.
  • Enhance Sovereignty: Support initiatives that increase Global South nations' monetary, food, and energy sovereignty—empowering regions to invest in their communities and lessen external dependencies.

Reducing Material and Energy Used in Production and Consumption

  • Scale Down Harmful Industries: Equitably reduce the size of industries harmful to the environment, like fossil fuels, industrial animal agriculture, and fast fashion.
  • End Planned Obsolescence: Encourage the design of durable, repairable products to reduce waste and extend product lifespans.
  • Cut Advertising: Reallocate advertising budgets towards promoting sustainable practices and cultural activities, reducing consumerism.
  • Shift to Usership: Encourage models of sharing and renting instead of ownership to lessen the demand for production and reduce waste.
  • Money Creation: Support the principle that governments, rather than banks, should be the primary creators of money to finance public goods and diminish inequality.
  • Co-operative Models: Advocate for businesses to adopt co-operative and steward-ownership models, prioritising community welfare and environmental sustainability over profits.
  • Income Equality: Implement income equality measures, such as setting minimum and maximum wage limits, wage ratios and adopting progressive taxation.
  • Fossil Fuel Treaty: Commit to the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty to drastically cut reliance on fossil fuels.

A comprehensive review of degrowth literature revealed 530 specific policy proposals across 13 themes, such as energy, finance, and urban planning, offering an exhaustive degrowth policy toolbox. This study makes a rich array of degrowth strategies accessible, outlining clear goals, objectives, and instruments essential for guiding sustainable policy-making.

Embrace Diverse and Emancipatory Education
Promote emancipatory education that nurtures critical thinking, ecological awareness, and community involvement.

Degrowth Pedagogy Training: Develop comprehensive training programs for teachers, focusing on integrating degrowth concepts, critical thinking, and ecological awareness into their teaching methods.

Experiential Learning: Encourage hands-on learning experiences that connect students with their local environments and communities, such as community service projects, outdoor education, and permaculture design courses.

Eco-spirituality in Curricula: Incorporate eco-spirituality into educational content to explore the deep connections between ecological sustainability, personal well-being, and spiritual fulfilment.

Emotional Pedagogies: Implement teaching approaches that recognize and engage with the emotional dimensions of learning about sustainability and degrowth, fostering a compassionate and empathetic understanding of global challenges.

Foster Cultures of Sufficiency: Promote educational content and practices that highlight the value of enoughness and sustainable living. Key instruments include public education on sustainable lifestyle choices, converting schools into community-oriented learning spaces, and encouraging the slow food philosophy and minimalist living through educational programs.

Revitalize Indigenous and Local Knowledge: Incorporate and restore indigenous learning and traditional ecological knowledge within educational systems. This can be achieved through critical community and liberation psychology, spaces for creativity and reflection, and library activism that prioritizes indigenous narratives.

Build Ecological and Social Consciousness: Implement robust ecological education at all levels, emphasizing the interconnectedness of social and environmental wellbeing. This involves education for a steady-state and circular economy, empowerment through ecological and social justice education, and promoting pluralism in economics education.

Encourage Multilingualism and Cultural Awareness: Advocate for learning multiple languages and understanding diverse cultures to counteract English-language imperialism and foster global solidarity. Instruments for this objective could include language exchange programs, multicultural education initiatives, and resources that highlight cultural diversity.
